# KSP Transfer Planner Style Guide

## Teaching Voice

This is a Kaizen tool: it should teach, not just calculate. The tone should feel like a clever KSP parts description got promoted to mission control. Keep the explanations plain-English and useful, but let the gallows humor stay alive.

Good copy should:

- Explain the actual mechanism instead of hiding behind jargon.
- Treat jokes as teaching handles, not replacements for clarity.
- Use KSP-style disaster humor without making the page feel mean or careless.
- Prefer concrete mental models over abstract summaries.
- Make real orbital mechanics feel approachable enough to try in-game.

## Page Structure

Use terminal-style orange/typewriter headings for major page and section titles. Taglines should be smaller than the title and should support the section, not compete with it.

The root page pattern is:

- Hero: KSP-specific title and press art.
- What Is KSP?: plain-language introduction and project framing.
- Key Terms: begin with one full-width orbit hero, followed by compact glossary cards with technical diagrams where helpful. On desktop, the hero spans all four glossary columns with its technical image occupying the left two column units and its narrative copy on the right. On narrow screens, stack the image above the copy.
- Where to?: planner family cards.

The orbit hero is the glossary's conceptual anchor. It should explain orbit as continuous free fall along a geodesic, maneuvers as changes from one orbit to another, and the familiar mental model of throwing yourself sideways fast enough to keep missing the ground. Its technical sequence runs from launch through insertion and circularization, then adds three visibly distinct patched flight segments: a hot-pink lunar transfer into a close gravity brake, a purple free return out of that flyby, and a white atmospheric re-entry leg. Place the launch site due south of the planet and use a shallow, balanced parabolic ascent that leaves along the local vertical before joining the insertion-orbit tangent. The launch leg must remain fully inside the insertion trajectory until their shared node; it may touch there but must never cross the insertion orbit. Avoid a deep or side-skewed arc that makes the launch leg read as a separate orbit.

The lunar transfer must leave the circular orbit tangentially and remain outside that current orbit after the prograde burn. Draw it as one smooth, expanding hot-pink curve: no flattening inside the green circle, no post-burn dip, no kink, no left turn, no chicane, and no wobble. Hot pink ends at the Moon-dominance intercept craft. From intercept through the close lunar arc to the pre-free-return craft, use yellow for the gravity-brake phase; only the outbound/top-left phase becomes purple at the tangent-continuous free-return patch. Construct the pink transfer and the yellow approach from one master curve split at the intercept, so the color handoff preserves position, tangent, and curvature rather than merely touching at a shared point. Place an approved hot-pink tail at intercept and a yellow tail ending at the gravity-brake craft. Do not attach a gravity vector to the intercept craft. Preserve the Moon-pointing vector on the purple free-return craft, with its stem visually connected beneath the craft and its tip stopping exactly at the Moon surface. Keep the flyby close enough to read as a deliberate lunar slingshot while preserving unmistakable surface clearance. The purple free return must leave the lunar arc on its exact tangent, sweep toward Earth as one broad monotonic curve, and remain clear of both bodies. Its handoff to the white atmospheric path must behave like two tangent orbital circles: shared position, travel direction, and curvature at the color patch, with no reversal or decorative wiggle. The white segment then tightens smoothly and meets the planet on the local surface tangent between the insertion and launch nodes; never kink a trajectory, aim it through a body, or turn re-entry into a hard radial dive. Every trajectory remains dotted, every visible phase carries the approved 16-part directional tail, every actual maneuver node repeats the craft, and every burn arrow inherits the color of the trajectory created by that burn. Keep the mechanics explicit in the drawing and let the ground's increasingly frustrated appointment schedule carry the joke.

Planner family drawings form a visual curriculum rather than three unrelated illustrations. Single-planet and interplanetary cards should repeat one physically legible transfer idea at different scales; the advanced card may become stranger, but its path must remain one continuous, mechanically interpretable trajectory. Use the same dark plotting field, restrained grid, teal bodies, blue reference geometry, orange craft/burn treatment, explicit tangent alignment, and 16-segment motion-tail convention established by the glossary diagrams. Advanced-maneuver humor should come from a dangerously plausible path—close passes, resonance loops, repeated assists, and visibly poor margins—not from disconnected doodles or joke labels. Every joined maneuver curve must maintain tangent continuity, clear every solid body, and distinguish an unpowered gravity assist from a burn; do not add radial guide spokes, orthogonal construction lines, or burn ticks that are not part of the actual maneuver.

Avoid "director's notes" copy that explains the page structure from outside the experience. The page should speak as the tool, not about the tool.

## Interactive Planner Labs

Planner labs inherit the homepage system; they are not a separate glass-dashboard product. Use the same dark star field, `8px` technical panels, teal-blue borders, amber Courier Prime headings with terminal-block accents, Space Grotesk supporting headings, and DM Sans teaching copy. Number the working sequence in the interface (`01 // inputs`, `02 // plot`, `03 // readouts`) so the calculator reads like a flight procedure instead of a generic analytics dashboard.

Keep the calculator mechanics visually explicit. Same-body Hohmann plots use cyan for the current orbit, amber for the transfer conic, green for the target orbit, and orange-with-cream-outline craft markers. These trajectory colors are informational and independent from maneuver-direction colors. Orbit strokes are restrained dotted technical paths rather than neon rings. Draw the traveled half of the transfer ellipse stronger than its unflown continuation. Burn craft must sit on the conic nodes and point along the local orbit tangent. Burn vectors are separate from the craft and use navball maneuver green `#4cff4c` for both prograde and retrograde; never key a lab burn arrow to the orbit it creates. Every burn node also carries the matching established navball marker image and a live `BURN N / Δv` note sourced from the calculator result. Use the raw marker glyph without a badge, border, background tile, or decorative accent. Place the glyph horizontally beside the craft, perpendicular to the vertical burn vector—not beyond the vector arrowhead. Stack Burn 1's note above its glyph and Burn 2's below; flip the complete group to the craft's other side only when the preferred outward side would clip. The current-orbit tail ends at burn 1 and the transfer tail ends at burn 2, each using the approved 16-segment, approximately `100deg` taper. Reference rings and grid lines stay subordinate to the actual trajectories.

Inputs, toggles, readouts, lesson cards, and callouts use crisp borders and very limited glow. Active controls use amber; live numeric outputs use cyan; the primary mission total uses amber. Keep keyboard focus visible. On narrow screens, place the plot before controls and readouts, preserve a usable canvas height, stack the lesson cards, and prevent all controls or text from overflowing the viewport.

Inclination-change labs reuse the same shell but change the technical grammar to plane geometry. Keep the current circular orbit cyan and key the target plane, burn vector, maneuver icon, and live burn note to navball hot pink `#ff18b0`. Project both orbits from their absolute signed plane angles against the same faint zero-degree reference plane: each slider runs from `-180deg` at left through `0deg` at center to `+180deg` at right, the current control moves only cyan, the target control moves only hot pink, and the two projected circles always retain their shared ascending and descending nodes. The signed scale is a planning shorthand around the displayed node line; KSP's standard inclination readout remains `0..180deg`. Calculate the shortest signed rotation, so `-180deg` and `+180deg` represent the same retrograde plane and a boundary crossing never creates a fake `360deg` burn. Never normalize cyan to zero and make both controls appear to move the target. Put the craft on the selected shared node, tangent to the current orbit, and show exactly one normal or anti-normal burn. Positive rotation uses normal at the ascending node and anti-normal at the descending node; negative rotation reverses that rule. The raw normal/anti-normal icon and live delta-v note sit beside the craft with no badge. Use the approved 16-segment tail on the current plane only. Inclination labs expose AN and DN node selection; do not carry over Hohmann's Swap control. Preserve the planet-inside-the-ring depth treatment, keep the angle annotation subordinate, and state plainly that plane changes are cheapest where orbital speed is lowest.

Combined transfer and plane-change labs merge those two grammars without muddying the maneuver components. Keep the current orbit cyan and transfer conic amber, but render the inclined target orbit in the normal/anti-normal hot pink `#ff18b0`; navball green remains reserved for tangential prograde/retrograde instructions. Keep the complete orbital system in the established horizontal Saturn-ring view. Rotate the live planes around the front/back orbital axis so their shared nodes sit on the vertical midline: the near node below the body and the far node above it. Never tilt the whole canvas to relocate the nodes. Mark both target-plane crossings, with the selected arrival node emphasized and the opposite AN/DN node subordinate. Burn 1 remains one navball-green prograde or retrograde instruction. At Burn 2, calculate the single velocity-vector difference between the arriving transfer and the tilted target circular orbit; do not add a circularization burn and an independent plane-change burn. Show its tangential component in navball green and its normal or anti-normal component in hot pink, with a thin cream resultant and the two established raw marker glyphs beside the craft. Both craft markers sit at exact shared conic nodes and follow local tangents. Use the approved 16-segment tail on the current orbit into Burn 1 and on the transfer conic into Burn 2. Signed plane controls run `-180deg..0deg..+180deg`, AN/DN selection changes only the normal-axis instruction, and the readouts compare the combined plan with circularizing then changing plane at the target—never with an invented global optimum. The teaching point is explicit: combining velocity changes saves fuel because vectors add geometrically, and making the plane component where speed is lower usually makes the budget hurt less.

Synchronous-orbit labs teach timing before they teach altitude. Compute the target radius from the selected body's exact sidereal rotation period and distinguish a merely geosynchronous orbit from a geostationary one: stationary requires circular, equatorial, prograde, and synchronous together. Keep the parking orbit cyan, transfer amber, and synchronous target green; maneuver components retain navball green and hot pink independently of those orbit colors. Stationary mode fixes inclination at zero and may use one thin, explicitly labeled longitude-lock alignment between the surface and arrival craft. Inclined synchronous mode removes that lock, tilts only the target plane, and may show one separate green figure-eight ground-track inset; never draw that ground track as a space trajectory. Validate the computed radius against the selected body's sphere of influence and show an unmistakable coral failure state when the target lies outside it. Do not hide the failure, round the sidereal period to the UI clock, or call equal period alone stationary. Reuse the established Hohmann/combined burn grammar, exact craft tangents, raw navball glyphs, and approved 16-segment tails.

Resonant-constellation labs use timing to create spacing. Keep the final circular orbit green, the carrier phasing ellipse amber, and the deployed satellite pips yellow-green. An inner `(N - 1):N` carrier orbit and outer `(N + 1):N` carrier orbit must share one exact tangent apsis with the target circle; the body remains at the physical focus and the opposite apsis comes from the live period ratio rather than hand-drawn geometry. Inner deployment circularizes prograde at apoapsis; outer deployment circularizes retrograde at periapsis, with navball-green arrows and the raw maneuver glyph beside the release craft. Show the approved 16-segment tail into the release node and into one deployed target-orbit craft, keep all craft tangent, and use a restrained phase arc to label the `360deg / N` slot rather than adding decorative spokes. Treat body, atmosphere, and SOI crossings as coral failure states with theoretical-only values. Controls choose the body, final altitude, craft count, and phasing side; the tool computes the resonant apsis, release interval, burn, slot angle, campaign time, and stated-assumption delta-v budget.

Repeat-ground-track labs plot surface revisit geometry rather than another space ellipse. Derive the circular altitude from the selected body's exact sidereal period and the reduced integer ratio of orbital revolutions to body rotations. Draw a real body-fixed equirectangular trace from the selected inclination, split cleanly at the dateline, and tint only the reachable latitude band. Keep the graticule subordinate, the full reduced-cycle track cyan and dashed, the repeat node hot pink, and the approved 16-segment direction tail yellow-green into an orange craft tangent to the local trace. A thin equator arrow may label the westward node shift per orbit, but do not add center spokes or decorative coverage rays. Inclination changes latitude reach and prograde/polar/retrograde direction without changing the period-derived altitude. Reduce duplicate ratios explicitly and show coral theoretical-only states for body, atmosphere, and SOI failures. Controls choose body, orbit count, body-rotation count, and inclination; altitude remains a computed result.

Polar-mapping labs invert that relationship: altitude and inclination are mission inputs, while surface coverage and cadence are results. Keep the body-fixed equirectangular plot, but render a finite sequence of near-polar ground tracks instead of forcing an integer repeat. Each pass uses a restrained translucent cyan swath behind a dashed cyan centerline; split every pass at the dateline and never connect the wrap with a false chord. The selected inclination controls latitude reach, the generic scanner swath controls how much surface each pass covers, and the body's exact sidereal rotation determines the westward longitude peel between passes. Show the approved 16-segment yellow-green tail into one orange craft tangent to the latest displayed ground track. Use hot pink only for the remaining largest equatorial gap and coral for unmapped polar caps or invalid orbit limits. Simulate successive half-orbit equator crossings until the largest circular gap fits inside one swath; do not estimate completion from `360deg / swath` alone or claim global coverage when the orbit misses either pole. Controls choose body, circular altitude, signed-near-polar inclination, and full scanner-swath angle. Readouts include safety, latitude reach, longitude peel, estimated mapping time, required passes, period, speed, equatorial swath width, and prograde/polar/retrograde direction.

## Technical SVG Diagrams

Glossary and planner diagrams should be precise SVG technical drawings, not decorative illustrations.

This is how orbital drawings should be treated throughout the project: technical drawing first, KSP flavor second. The geometry has to be right before the image gets cute.

Hard rule: do not edit a technical SVG diagram from intuition. Before changing diagram markup, update `diagram-spec.md` with the coordinate-level plan for the affected card, including current geometry, delta geometry, craft position, craft rotation, burn vector, marker badge reservation, and every visible stroke. If a visible mark cannot be justified by that spec, delete it. If the mechanics are uncertain, stop and ask instead of inventing a plausible-looking drawing.

Before coding a diagram:

- Update and read `diagram-spec.md` for the card being changed.
- State the coordinate plan.
- Define the `viewBox`.
- Define the orbit geometry explicitly with coordinates and dimensions.
- Place nodes on the actual geometry, not by visual guesswork.
- Calculate tangent-aligned craft markers from the geometry.
- Keep labels anchored to meaningful nodes or regions.
- Render-check the diagram at card size before calling it done.

Rules for orbit diagrams:

- Use SVG primitives and explicit path geometry.
- Prefer one clean orbit path over layered approximations.
- If a direction cue is needed, style the trajectory itself rather than attaching stray lines to the craft.
- For burn-direction cards, key the changed orbit, resulting-vector arrow, and icon treatment to the KSP marker color for that burn direction. Prograde uses marker green; carry the same marker-color logic through retrograde, normal, anti-normal, radial in, and radial out.
- Use top-down orbit views for prograde, retrograde, radial in, and radial out. Use an edge-on ecliptic/plane view for normal and anti-normal so the plane-change direction is visible instead of pretending it is an altitude change.
- Reserve the navball marker badge first, then draft the orbit around that reserved space. Do not let the craft, burn arrow, orbit node, or labels crowd the marker badge; move the craft to the opposite side when needed.
- Use tapered/fading trajectory cues only when they clarify the motion without cluttering the card. If a cue is used, it belongs on the actual current trajectory and is anchored to the craft geometry: thickest where it meets the craft at the exhaust side, then thinning and fading along the orbit until it is thinnest farthest behind the craft. Do not use generic left-to-right gradients, do not reverse the taper, and do not paint broad decorative strokes across the diagram. Radial cards and both plane-change cards use the approved orbital tail on their original/current orbit; do not add a tail to target plane-change geometry.
- For retrograde, mirror the prograde composition when the marker badge would crowd the craft: put the craft on the opposite side, reverse the tangent burn, and lower the opposite side.
- For normal, establish the original orbit as a shallow horizontal blue ellipse centered on the body, using the same Saturn-ring proportion as the anti-normal target ellipse. Put the craft at the foreground midpoint, centered directly in front of the planet and tangent to the ring, with the approved orbital tail ending at the craft. Redraw the lower-right foreground quadrant over the body and connect it directly to the craft's nose so the ship and near side of the orbit read in front of the planet. The hot-pink dashed target orbit uses the normal icon's exact `#ff18b0`, keeps the original ellipse dimensions, and rotates `-30deg` around the ship—not the planet—so its right side rises, its left side falls, and both orbits share the ship node. Split the target orbit into depth layers exactly like a Saturn ring: render the full target ellipse behind the planet, then redraw only its lower/front half over the planet. The upper/rear arm must disappear behind the body so the planet reads inside the target orbit. Draw the ship above both orbit layers.
- Anti-normal is normal with current and target roles reversed: the blue inclined ring is the original orbit, the hot-pink `#ff18b0` dashed horizontal midline is the target orbit, the ship remains at their shared foreground node, and the thrust arrow points due south. Reverse both normal translation curves so their stems begin on the inclined orbit and their arrowheads terminate on the horizontal midline. Use the exact normal trajectory-arrow marker construction so every arrowhead axis follows its terminal tangent and its flat back is perpendicular to the stem. Use the same `94×6` ellipses, `-30deg` ship-pivoted inclination, `translate(18 0)` system shift, Saturn-ring front/rear occlusion, approved 16-segment tail on the inclined current orbit, and top-layer craft treatment as normal. Rotate the craft `-30deg` so its nose points up and right, tangent to the original orbit.
- Normal uses one solid `#ff18b0` burn arrow pointing due north from the ship, with a `56.4`-unit stem. It also uses paired blue-to-hot-pink translation arrows at the original orbit's extreme left and right points; each stem starts on the original orbit and bows gently outward, away from the planet, before connecting to the corresponding target-orbit extreme. Align every translation arrowhead to its terminal stem tangent: the arrowhead axis continues the stem and its flat back edge is perpendicular to that stem. Do not rotate an arrowhead to match or oppose the target orbit. Keep the normal badge fixed and translate the complete technical drawing `18` viewBox units right as one rigid group, preserving every internal anchor and proportion. Use butt caps on the normal vector stems and seat the burn stem endpoint beneath its arrowhead (`refX=8`) so no circular terminal artifact remains visible. The craft is the top layer above both orbits and all vectors.
- For radial in/out, do not delete the orbital consequence just because it is subtler than prograde/retrograde. Show the burn vector along the planet-craft radius and include a cautious dashed delta orbit that shares the burn point while rotating/reshaping the path; avoid implying a simple apoapsis raise/lower. Add a second result arrow at the ecliptic between the current and delta orbits: radial out belongs on the right and points right; radial in belongs on the left and points left. In both cases the stem starts at the current orbit and the arrow terminates at the new orbit.
- Do not place craft markers off the trajectory.
- Do not let periapsis intersect the body unless the concept is impact.
- Do not add decorative guide lines unless they teach something specific.
- Avoid emoji for technical markers unless explicitly requested.
- Keep effects restrained; no glow-heavy decoration that obscures the geometry.

### Approved Orbital Tail Standard

Orbital tails are an established technical convention, not a per-card styling choice.

- Apoapsis, periapsis, prograde, retrograde, radial out, radial in, normal, and anti-normal must each use 16 contiguous short arc segments covering approximately `100deg` of the actual current orbit and ending at the craft. Do not shorten any of these tails to save space.
- Prograde runs from approximately `t=80deg` to `t=180deg` on its current circle. Retrograde runs from approximately `t=-100deg` to `t=0deg` on its current ellipse. Radial out and radial in both run from approximately `t=-190deg` to `t=-90deg` on their current circles. Normal and anti-normal both use local current-ellipse segments from approximately `t=190deg` to `t=90deg`; anti-normal rotates the complete tail `-30deg` with its inclined current orbit. These ranges are deliberate motion cues.
- Use the same taper on all eight cards. From `tail-1` through `tail-16`, approved stroke widths are `0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 1.1, 1.3, 1.55, 1.8, 2.05, 2.35, 2.65, 2.95, 3.25, 3.55, 3.85, 4.15, 4.5px`; approved opacities are `0.04, 0.08, 0.13, 0.19, 0.26, 0.34, 0.43, 0.52, 0.61, 0.70, 0.78, 0.84, 0.89, 0.93, 0.96, 0.98`.
- The tail is thinnest and faintest farthest behind the craft, then becomes thickest and most opaque where it meets the craft. Every segment must sit on the orbit geometry; never detach, reverse, or replace it with a gradient stroke.
- When changing orbit geometry, recalculate all segment endpoints from the orbit equation while preserving the approved segment count, angular coverage, direction, and taper.

Approved Apoapsis card geometry:

- `viewBox="0 0 320 210"`
- Orbit ellipse: `cx=160`, `cy=105`, `rx=112`, `ry=92`
- Planet at the left focus: `(97, 105)`
- Planet radius: `34`
- Periapsis clears the surface because the left vertex is at `x=48`, leaving about `15px` of clearance from the planet edge.
- Apoapsis node at the right vertex: `(272, 105)`
- Craft marker on the ellipse near `t=-35deg`: approximately `(252, 52)`
- Craft marker tangent angle: approximately `50deg`
- Direction highlight is 16 short arc segments on the same ellipse from about `t=-135deg` to `t=-35deg`, using the approved orbital-tail taper.
