Flight manual // Orbital vegetable peeler

The orbit stays put. The map moves.

A polar orbital plane is nearly fixed in inertial space while the body turns beneath it. Each north-south lap therefore crosses a different longitude. Given enough time and enough scanner width, those passes peel the surface into mapped strips—assuming the probe survives the traditional transition from “fully autonomous” to “why is it not responding?”

Inclination sets latitude

A 90° orbit crosses both poles. An 80° orbit stops at 80° north and south unless the scanner is wide enough to see the remaining caps sideways. Near-polar is not a synonym for global when the last ten degrees are where the anomaly lives.

Altitude sets the peel

Higher circular orbits take longer, so the body rotates farther beneath each lap. That changes the longitude spacing between tracks. Altitude therefore controls both orbital cadence and how aggressively the survey pattern walks west.

Swath closes the gaps

The centerline is only where the spacecraft passes; the instrument maps a strip on either side. This planner waits until the largest equatorial gap fits inside one full swath instead of dividing 360° by a hopeful number and declaring science complete.

What this is good for

Use polar survey orbits for biome hunting, resource scans, KerbNet coverage, or proving that the suspiciously smooth patch near the pole was absolutely intentional and not a terrain-generation conversation nobody wants to reopen.

Kerbal field note

The swath control is a generic surface-angle model, not a promise about one particular scanner part. Match it to your instrument and acceptable overlap, then verify the first few tracks in map view before time-warping through the entire mission and discovering the probe was mounted upside down.
