Flight manual // Same planet, same footprints

Repeat does not mean stationary

A repeat-ground-track orbit synchronizes two clocks: orbital revolutions and body rotations. The spacecraft crosses many different longitudes during the cycle, then the entire pattern closes and starts again. It revisits a route, not one permanent parking space; the distinction matters to both cartographers and increasingly irritated antenna operators.

The ratio sets altitude

Six orbits per Kerbin day means each lap must last exactly one-sixth of the sidereal rotation period. Kepler then determines one circular radius. Altitude is the result, not another free slider you can move until the answer becomes emotionally convenient.

Inclination sets reach

A circular orbit reaches latitudes equal to its effective inclination. A 70° orbit covers between 70° north and south; a polar orbit can cross every latitude. Changing inclination changes the shape of the trace but not the repeat timing or altitude.

The pattern must close

After each lap, planetary rotation shifts the next pass westward. When the orbit and rotation counts are whole numbers, the final pass returns to the first node. If both counts share a divisor, the actual pattern closes early and Mission Control has been padding the schedule with duplicate laps.

What this is good for

Use repeat tracks for KerbNet surveys, resource scanners, predictable relay passes, or revisiting the same landing corridor under similar geometry. It is excellent when change over time matters and less excellent when you urgently need coverage during the eleven hours between scheduled appearances.

Kerbal field note

Use the body's sidereal rotation period, not the time shown by a sunrise. Set the orbit, let it run through the full reduced cycle, and compare the first and last equator crossing in map view. If they miss, the period is wrong; if the map is rotating because somebody grabbed the camera, the experiment is temporarily inconclusive.
