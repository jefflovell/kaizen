Flight manual // Read before turning north

What is a plane change?

Inclination is the angle between an orbit and its reference plane. A plane-change burn rotates the velocity vector at a node while keeping the spacecraft at the same place. The orbit pivots around the line joining the ascending and descending nodes; it does not climb to a new altitude just because the map view became dramatic.

The node is the hinge

The old and new planes intersect at two points. Burn at one of those nodes so your current position belongs to both orbits. Burning elsewhere is how a clean geometry lesson becomes an expensive three-dimensional apology.

Why speed hurts

For equal-speed circular orbits, the cost is 2v sin(Δi/2). The faster you are moving, the more velocity must be turned. That is why plane changes are usually cheaper high in the orbit, where speed is lower and Mission Control has more time to stare at the remaining fuel.

Normal versus anti-normal

  • Normal points toward the navball's north pole.
  • Anti-normal points toward the south pole.
  • The correct marker flips when you use the opposite node.
  • It also flips when the signed plane rotation reverses.

Eyeball it in KSP

Add a maneuver at the AN or DN marker, then pull normal or anti-normal until the target inclination is reached. Watch the opposite node collapse toward zero degrees; if it grows, congratulations, you selected the correct axis with inspiring confidence and the wrong sign.

Launch into it first

The cheapest plane change is the one you never perform. Launch azimuth and launch-site latitude can place much of the required inclination into the ascent, paid for while you were already spending fuel and alarming everyone near the space center.

Model limits

  • This lab assumes an instantaneous burn in a circular orbit.
  • The signed scale describes rotation around this displayed node line; KSP reports inclination from 0° to 180°.
  • It changes plane only; altitude and orbital speed magnitude stay fixed.
  • Large real burns should be centered on the node.
  • Combined altitude and plane changes can cost less than separate burns.
Plane-change survival rule

Change planes where you are slow, combine the turn with another burn when practical, and launch closer to the plane you wanted in the first place. Delta-v is not angry; it is simply keeping receipts.
