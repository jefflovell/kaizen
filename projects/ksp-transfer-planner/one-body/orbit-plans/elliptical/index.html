Flight manual // The long way around

One orbit, two very different neighborhoods.

Periapsis and apoapsis are not two destinations connected by a line; they are the nearest and farthest points of one continuous free-fall path. Set both and the rest of the ellipse follows: its size, shape, period, and the speed at which the terrain team begins using phrases like “unacceptably proximal.”

Periapsis is fast

Falling inward converts gravitational potential into speed. The craft reaches its maximum velocity at periapsis, which makes close science passes exciting and terrain clearance discussions suddenly very specific.

Apoapsis is slow

The same craft crawls at apoapsis and spends more of every orbit near the high end. That dwell time is useful for communications and observation, or for postponing the return to the consequences waiting at periapsis.

The second burn is optional

Burn once from a circular orbit and the ellipse is already a complete orbit. The opposite burn shown here circularizes only if the mission wants to abandon the ellipse. Skip it and the spacecraft keeps cycling between the selected apsides.

Mind the boundaries

A periapsis below the surface is an impact trajectory. A periapsis inside an atmosphere is an aerobraking pass—or a re-entry, depending on how convincingly the heat shield was described in the parts list. An apoapsis outside the SOI is not a stable one-body ellipse.

Kerbal field note

Burn direction changes the opposite apsis. Prograde at periapsis raises apoapsis; retrograde at apoapsis lowers periapsis. If the wrong marker starts moving, stop pulling the handle and reconsider the strongly held belief that map view was facing north.
