Flight manual // One burn, two axes, zero excuses

Why combine the burns?

A combined maneuver changes speed and direction at the same instant. Because those velocity changes are vectors, the required burn is the diagonal of the component triangle—not the full length of both sides added together. Mission Control calls this efficiency. The fuel tank calls it a temporary stay of execution.

The vector triangle

The green component changes your speed along the orbit. The hot-pink component rotates the velocity vector into the new plane. Burn them together and the engine follows the resultant between them, spending less delta-v than two independent burns.

Slow is merciful

Plane changes get cheaper as orbital speed falls. For an orbit raise, apoapsis is both the circularization point and the slow end of the transfer, making it the natural place to tilt the plane while everyone still has propellant to argue about.

The node is the hinge

Perform the combined burn where the old transfer plane intersects the target plane. Choose AN or DN to flip the normal-axis instruction; the ideal delta-v is the same, but the navball marker absolutely is not, and KSP notices these details at speed.

What this model assumes

The calculator assumes instantaneous burns, circular start and target orbits, and a target-plane node exactly at transfer arrival. Long burns, eccentric starting orbits, and missed nodes require proper mission planning instead of louder optimism.

Kerbal field note

Create the arrival node, add the tangential component first, then pull normal or anti-normal until the target orbit and node line agree. If the maneuver gizmo looks like a frightened sea urchin, pause before adding another handle.
