Flight manual // Rotational tax assessment

The planet was already helping. You said no.

A rotating launch site already has eastward inertial velocity. Launching prograde keeps that gift; launching retrograde must cancel it and then build the same speed westward. The orbit itself is perfectly respectable. The invoice is for arguing with the ground before you ever leave it.

Inclination includes direction

Zero degrees is equatorial prograde, 90° is polar, and anything beyond 90° is retrograde. At 180° the orbital plane is still equatorial, but the spacecraft travels west while the surface turns east beneath it.

Latitude limits the launch

A direct launch cannot reach an inclination closer to the equator than the launch site’s latitude. The retrograde mirror is bounded the same way: from 90° up to 180° minus latitude. Beyond that, launch into a reachable plane and pay for a plane change later.

What the penalty means

The rotation penalty compares this westbound insertion with an otherwise identical eastbound orbit mirrored across 90°. It isolates the cost of rejecting surface rotation; gravity drag, aerodynamic drag, steering, and all other ascent-shaped panic are deliberately excluded.

Why do it anyway?

Retrograde geometry can support unusual rendezvous, coverage, contracts, and role-playing decisions made by administrators who saw “high relative velocity” and assumed that meant the mission was going faster in a productive sense.

Kerbal field note

This is a clean velocity-vector comparison at orbital insertion, not a full ascent simulator. Add your normal launch losses—and a generous allowance for realizing the heading was east after staging the boosters—to estimate the actual mission budget.
