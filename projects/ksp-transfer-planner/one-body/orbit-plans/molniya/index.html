Flight manual // Geostationary looked south

Race low. Loiter high. Repeat before anyone notices.

A Molniya-style orbit trades constant equatorial visibility for long, slow passes over one high-latitude hemisphere. The spacecraft sprints through low periapsis, climbs to a deliberately parked apoapsis, and spends most of the clock moving slowly where the mission actually needs coverage.

The ellipse buys time

Kepler gives equal areas in equal times, so the craft must crawl through the broad apoapsis end and race through the narrow periapsis end. High eccentricity turns that speed difference into useful dwell instead of merely an alarming velocity readout.

Half a day repeats

The classic orbit lasts half a sidereal day. Apoapsis alternates across the planet each revolution and returns to the same longitude pattern one day later, assuming Mission Control has not changed the definition of “same” during the debrief.

Why 63.4°?

On an oblate real planet, the critical inclination suppresses the drift of the line of apsides. Stock KSP does not model that J2 precession, so 63.4° is a useful real-world reference—not a magic stationkeeping coupon from the Kraken.

One craft still leaves

A single satellite eventually dives away from the coverage region. Continuous service normally uses multiple phased spacecraft so another arrives before the first one bolts through periapsis with the radios screaming in Doppler.

Kerbal field note

This idealized one-body calculator derives the resonant ellipse and time above the selected latitude. It does not include launch-to-plane cost, J2 precession, stationkeeping, terrain masking, or the meeting where somebody asks whether one dish can simply point “more north.”
