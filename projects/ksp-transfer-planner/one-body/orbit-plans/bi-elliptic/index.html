Flight manual // Cheapest is not shortest

Why go past the destination?

A bi-elliptic transfer joins two half-ellipses at one very high apoapsis. The first burn leaves the starting circle, the second reshapes the far side to meet the target circle, and the third circularizes. Moving that middle burn far from the body makes velocity cheap to change—but turns the mission clock into an archaeological record.

Three burns, two ellipses

Burn 1 raises the shared apoapsis. Burn 2 changes the opposite apsis until the second ellipse touches the target orbit. Burn 3 circularizes there. Every burn is tangent; nobody hangs a hard left in space because the diagram ran out of room.

The high burn is cheap

Orbital speed is lowest at the shared apoapsis. A small change there can move the opposite apsis by an enormous amount, which is the Oberth effect's quieter, spreadsheet-loving cousin.

11.94 is not a coupon

The famous radius ratio near 11.94 describes the limiting case as the intermediate apoapsis goes extremely far away. A finite apoapsis can still lose to Hohmann. This lab compares the actual burns instead of granting victory by slogan.

Time sends the invoice

Two long half-orbits can take vastly longer than one Hohmann coast. Saving a handful of meters per second is less thrilling when the crew has eaten the backup snacks and begun treating the life-support manual as speculative fiction.

Kerbal field note

This idealized planner assumes instantaneous tangential burns, circular coplanar endpoints, and a one-body patched-conic world. Long burns, rendezvous timing, perturbations, and crossing the sphere of influence remain available to ruin the meeting in more advanced ways.
