What is a Hohmann transfer?

A Hohmann transfer is the classic two-burn way to move between two circular, coplanar orbits around the same body. It is not magic, and it is not every transfer. It is a very tidy trick: use one burn to make an ellipse that just touches the target orbit, coast halfway around, then use a second burn to make the new orbit circular.

Forever missing the ground

Think of orbit like throwing a ball so fast that Earth curves away from it faster than gravity can force it to the ground. Without atmosphere to slow it down, and without mountains to crash into, the ball would free fall forever, moving fast enough to keep missing the ground. If you want to go farther out, you need to go faster. A prograde burn does not lift the ball straight up at the burn; it speeds the ball up, stretching the travel path so the far side of the orbit rises. You fall down farther past the horizon because you are moving forward faster. Burn once to stretch the orbit into an ellipse, coast to the raised side, then burn again to stretch the original side and turn the ellipse back into a circle.

Why it is useful

Hohmann transfers are usually fuel-efficient for clean circular orbit changes. That matters because delta-v is your mission budget, and the rocket equation is where cheerful payload dreams go to become spreadsheets.

What to watch for

  • Burn 1 changes the opposite side of the orbit.
  • Burn 2 circularizes at apoapsis or periapsis.
  • The transfer angle is 180 degrees for this same-body case.
  • Long burns smear the maneuver across the orbit.

Eyeball it in KSP

For a raise, burn prograde until the apoapsis marker reaches the target altitude. Coast there, then burn prograde again until the periapsis rises to match. For a lower orbit, reverse the idea: burn retrograde to drop the opposite side, coast, then circularize.

When not to use it

Do not worship the perfect Hohmann. If you need to dodge terrain, rendezvous with a target, change inclination, land safely, or get somewhere fast, a textbook transfer may be the wrong tool. Good mission design balances fuel cost, time, precision, safety, and payload capacity.

Pitfalls worth surviving

  • Do not start a long burn exactly at the node. Center it.
  • Low specific impulse and low thrust can make forever burns.
  • Do not confuse transfer angle with rendezvous phase angle.
  • Leave margin. KSP rewards confidence, then invoices it.
Don't let perfect win

A clean Hohmann is a reference move, not a moral obligation. If your orbit is close, your burn is short, and your mission still works, that is planning. The goal is not to impress Walter Hohmann. The goal is to arrive with fuel, payload, and a crew that has not started naming snacks after failed prototypes.
