Circular orbit raise / lower

“Two burns, one orbit, and only a modest chance someone asks why re-entry is now a philosophical problem.”

This is how spacecraft tidy up their orbit after launch, raise into a safer parking orbit, nudge a station back where it belongs, or move satellites into the shell where they can start doing their job.

Inclination change

“Turn north after launch, they said. It’ll be fine, they said. The recovery team has requested fewer optimists in mission planning.”

Polar and high-inclination satellites sweep over more of the planet as it spins underneath them, which makes them perfect for mapping, weather, ice tracking, and the kind of observation everyone pretends is mostly about clouds.

Combined transfer + plane change

“Do the altitude change and plane change together, because if we’re going to waste fuel, we may as well call it vector math.”

Real missions rarely get perfect textbook moments, so flight planners often blend altitude and plane changes into one burn to save fuel, time, or whatever dignity remains in the propellant budget.

Geosynchronous / geostationary

“Park politely above the same patch of dirt forever, for communications, weather, or the quiet geopolitical pressure of an orbital railgun throne.”

Geosynchronous satellites orbit once per planetary day, so they can keep showing up over the same region for communications, weather watching, broadcasting, and other why-is-that-thing-always-up-there services.

Resonant orbit

“Release satellites at mathematically pleasing intervals until the whole world wonders if the shape is intentional and the tracking station stops asking follow-up questions.”

Satellite constellations use orbital timing like a clockwork bead necklace, spacing vehicles around a shared orbit so coverage appears steady instead of clumped into one very expensive parade.

Repeat ground track

“Fly over the same place again and again, because mass surveillance sounds better when you call it coverage.”

Repeat ground tracks let a satellite revisit the same places on a schedule, which is useful for mapping change over time: crops, storms, roads, glaciers, construction sites, and anything else that gets nervous under a camera.

Polar mapping orbit

“See the whole planet eventually, including the parts the mission brief described as ‘outside scope’ shortly before losing contact.”

A polar mapper eventually sees almost every latitude because the planet rotates beneath its north-south path, turning one orbit track into a slow peel of the whole world.

Retrograde orbit

“How expensive is spite? Very.”

Retrograde orbits move against the planet’s spin, which costs extra energy but can be useful for special coverage, unusual science goals, or missions that care more about geometry than politeness.

Elliptical orbit designer

“Spend most of your time far away from your problems, then return to periapsis at a speed that suggests you should have just dealt with them like an adult.”

Elliptical orbits are useful when you want a spacecraft to dive close for speed or science, then coast far away for time, coverage, communications, or a dramatic pause before the next questionable decision.

Molniya-style orbit

“Linger dramatically over the high latitudes, because geostationary orbit refused to look north and Russia took that personally.”

Molniya-style orbits solve a real high-latitude problem: geostationary satellites sit near the equator, so highly elliptical tilted orbits can hang over northern or southern regions for much longer each pass.

Bi-elliptic transfer

“What if we went absurdly far away first? Sometimes genius, usually a lot of paperwork and several very carefully worded press releases.”

A bi-elliptic transfer uses three burns and a very high apoapsis to sometimes beat a Hohmann transfer on fuel for huge orbit changes, proving that go-farther-first is occasionally not a terrible sentence.

Launch site assist

“Launch from the right latitude and the planet helps. Launch from the wrong one and the planet becomes a very large, very smug physics professor.”

Launch sites get free sideways speed from the rotating planet, and the amount depends on latitude and launch direction, which is why equatorial launches are prized and high-latitude launches start with a lecture from geometry.
