Flight manual // Weaponized punctuality

Why the satellites spread out

A resonant orbit changes the carrier's period while preserving one shared deployment point with the final circular orbit. Every time the carrier returns to that point, the satellites already deployed have moved one exact slot farther around the circle. The carrier keeps the appointment; everybody else uses the time to stop standing in a pile.

Inner runs fast

The inner phasing orbit has a shorter period. For five satellites it uses a 4:5 resonance, returning after the target orbit advances four-fifths of a lap. Each released craft burns prograde at apoapsis to join the final circle. Check periapsis before discovering that your clockwork relay dispenser is also an atmosphere probe.

Outer runs slow

The outer option uses a 6:5 resonance for five satellites. The carrier takes longer, so each released craft circularizes retrograde at periapsis. This costs similar velocity but trades the low-altitude hazard for a higher orbit and a longer campaign.

One slot per lap

Leave one satellite in the final orbit, move the carrier onto the resonant ellipse, then release and circularize one craft every carrier lap. After the final release, the constellation is evenly spaced by 360° divided by the craft count—assuming nobody fat-fingered time warp during the thirty-second window.

What this is good for

Use resonance to build relay rings, evenly timed scanner passes, or navigation constellations from one launch stack. It replaces eyeballing separation with orbital timing, which is useful because “that looks like about seventy-two degrees” is not a recognized unit at Mission Control.

Kerbal field note

The burn shown is the ideal impulse at the shared apsis. In KSP, decouple first, switch to the released satellite, circularize at the node, and return to the carrier before it wanders away. Name the craft before deployment unless “Relay Probe” repeated twelve times is the constellation-management experience you were hoping for.
