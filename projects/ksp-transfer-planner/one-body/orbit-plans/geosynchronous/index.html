Flight manual // Same clock, different consequences

What actually stays still?

Geosynchronous means the orbit matches the body's sidereal day. The satellite returns to the same place in its schedule after one rotation, but it may roam north, south, east, or west in between. Geostationary is the special case that actually stays over one longitude: synchronous, circular, equatorial, and prograde. Same clock; very different promise to the people aiming antennas at it.

Same day, not same place

Every synchronous orbit uses the same period and semi-major axis. Inclination makes the craft wander north and south; eccentricity makes it speed up, slow down, and wander east and west. It comes back after one sidereal day, but your dish may spend the hours between appointments staring into space and reconsidering its career.

Stationary: hold this longitude

Use a stationary orbit when one satellite must remain continuously visible from the same equatorial region. Around Kerbin, that makes a good relay anchor above the KSC, an always-on link for an equatorial base, or one fixed corner of a three-satellite network. Circular, equatorial, prograde, and synchronous are all mandatory.

Synchronous: repeat the coverage

Use an inclined synchronous orbit when repeating geometry matters more than hovering. It can revisit high-latitude bases on the same daily schedule, alternate coverage with other relays, or create predictable communication windows. The map-view ground track becomes a figure eight: useful rhythm, not continuous service.

Pick the promise

Need one dish pointed at one relay all day? Choose stationary. Need a relay to return over the same base every day after roaming elsewhere? Choose synchronous. Need polar coverage without gaps? Use multiple craft or a different orbit family; one inclined synchronous satellite cannot be everywhere just because the schedule is tidy.

Kerbal field note

Practical test: warp through one sidereal day in map view. A stationary relay remains over the same terrain the entire time. A merely synchronous relay moves across the map and returns at the end. If it never returns, the period is wrong. If the calculated rail lies outside the SOI, the body has declined to participate regardless of antenna size.
