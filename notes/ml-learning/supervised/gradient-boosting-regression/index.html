Gradient Boosting Regression

Instead of averaging independent trees, boosting builds a sequence: each small tree corrects mistakes left by the current model.

Business problem

Can a rough demand forecast be improved one mistake at a time?

Situation

A simple average misses hot occupied homes, efficient solar homes, and other recurring pockets of error.

Problem to solve

The forecasting team needs to capture subtle structured patterns without growing one enormous, brittle tree.

Why gradient boosting

Each small tree learns part of the remaining residual, so the model improves through controlled sequential corrections.

Industry contextOften the tabular model to beat

Gradient-boosted trees are industry norms for revenue forecasting, demand prediction, pricing, and other structured-data problems. They frequently outperform deep networks on tables, but learning rate, tree depth, leakage, and early stopping require careful validation.

Suppose the first energy forecast predicts the same average demand for every home. It will underestimate hot, occupied homes and overestimate efficient homes with rooftop solar. Those remaining gaps are residuals.

Gradient boosting trains a small decision tree to predict those residuals, adds a fraction of its correction to the model, then repeats. The ensemble improves step by step—more like an editor revising a draft than a committee voting all at once.

  • Deeper trees and a high learning rate reach that overfitting region sooner.

Random forest versus gradient boosting

Parallel opinions versus sequential revisions.

A random forest grows varied trees independently and averages their predictions. Gradient boosting grows trees in order: Tree 2 depends on the mistakes left by Tree 1, Tree 3 depends on the ensemble before it, and so on.

Random forests primarily reduce variance through averaging. Boosting primarily reduces bias by repeatedly correcting systematic errors—but can overfit if the sequence grows too aggressive or too long.

How it is built

Measure the miss. Learn the miss. Add a careful correction.

The demonstration begins at the mean, trains each weak tree on the current residuals, and scales every correction by the learning rate.

01 · Begin with the mean
F0(x) = ȳ

F0 (“F sub zero”) is the initial model. ȳ (“y-bar”) is the mean actual demand across the training homes.

02 · Calculate residuals
ri,m = yi − Fm−1(xi)

ri,m (“r sub i, m”) is example i’s residual at round m: actual demand minus the previous ensemble prediction.

03 · Add the next tree
Fm(x) = Fm−1(x) + ηhm(x)

hm (“h sub m”) is the new tree’s residual correction. η (“eta”) scales how much of that correction joins the ensemble.

Key terms

The vocabulary of iterative improvement.

Boosting turns error into the target for the next learner, creating a sequence whose members are deliberately dependent.

Boosting
Building models sequentially so each new learner improves the current ensemble.
Residual
For squared-error regression, actual value minus the current prediction.
Weak learner
A deliberately simple model, often a shallow tree, that performs one modest correction.
Learning rate η (eta)
A hyperparameter controlling how much each new tree changes the ensemble.
Boosting round
One cycle of calculating residuals, fitting a tree, and adding its scaled correction.
Sequential ensemble
An ensemble whose later models depend on the outputs and errors of earlier models.
MAE
Mean Absolute Error: the average absolute distance between actual and predicted values.
Overfitting
Learning training-specific noise so aggressively that performance on new examples worsens.
