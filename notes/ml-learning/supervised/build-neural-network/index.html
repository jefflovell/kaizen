Build a Neural Network

Start with real-world features, turn them into numbers, and assemble one complete prediction from the inside out.

A neural network diagram can look like a mysterious web of circles and lines. It becomes much simpler when we build it one operation at a time.

The same object will grow throughout this lesson: features become a vector, the vector enters a neuron, bias and activation shape its response, and several neurons become a feedforward network.

What are we actually modeling?

One title. One audience. One prediction.

This is a stripped-down regression model. Its example is a title paired with an audience cohort—such as “families who often finish animated adventures.” Its target is the percentage of exposed viewers expected to complete the title during its first week.

It is not an end-to-end recommender. A real system might retrieve thousands of candidate titles, then use a score like this as one input to ranking. We abstract that surrounding system away so we can inspect the neural-network math.

Where do features come from?

What is it?

Features are facts we choose to tell the model about one example.

How does it work?

Raw observations are measured, cleaned, and converted into consistently ordered numeric inputs.

Why is it useful?

Features turn real-world evidence into numbers a model can compare consistently.

Keep these separate

A feature is input data. It is not something this small network learns during training.

The behavior layer, compressed

These numbers came from somewhere.

A production model would consume many raw events and learned embeddings. For this lesson, we compress that upstream behavior into three inspectable teaching features.

01 · Audience–title affinity

How likely is this cohort to care?

Could come from
Past watches, searches, clicks, completions, skips, likes, and similarity to related titles.
Why useful
Behavior often reveals preference more reliably than a survey or broad demographic label.
Why imperfect
New users and titles have little history; popularity and prior recommendations can reinforce themselves.
02 · Title awareness

Does the cohort know the title exists?

Could come from
Campaign reach, trailer views, impressions, searches, franchise familiarity, and talent recognition.
Why useful
People rarely start a title they never notice. Awareness can strongly affect early viewing.
Why imperfect
Awareness does not equal interest, and recommendations can themselves create awareness—making cause and effect messy.
03 · Tone preference match

Does the title feel like what this cohort enjoys?

Could come from
Content metadata or embeddings matched against prior behavior: lighthearted, serious, tense, comforting, fast, or slow.
Why useful
Two titles in the same genre can produce very different reactions because their emotional experience differs.
Why imperfect
Tone labels are subjective, tastes change by context, and one cohort score hides differences among individuals.
What was abstracted away?

Individual user sequences, item embeddings, device and session context, availability, novelty, household composition, candidate retrieval, exploration, business rules, and feedback loops.
