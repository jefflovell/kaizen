Random Forest Regression

One tree can overreact. A forest averages many different trees into a steadier prediction.

Business problem

How much energy will a home need tomorrow?

Situation

Demand depends on interacting thresholds across temperature, occupancy, insulation, time, and solar generation.

Problem to solve

One deep tree can fit those interactions but overreact to quirks in one training sample.

Why a random forest

Many deliberately varied trees average away some instability while preserving nonlinear, threshold-based reasoning.

Industry contextProduction norm for tabular forecasting

Random forests are strong, dependable choices for demand, pricing, revenue, and risk-exposure forecasts built from structured business data. They often beat neural networks on modest tables because they need less scaling and tuning and naturally capture threshold interactions.

How much electricity will a home use during tomorrow’s heat wave? One decision tree might focus heavily on temperature. Another might split first on occupancy, time of day, insulation, or solar generation.

A random forest trains many trees on different samples and feature choices, then averages their numeric predictions. Individual trees keep their useful nonlinear reasoning; averaging reduces the influence of any one tree’s peculiar opinion.

The question: Can a crowd of imperfect trees forecast tomorrow better than one highly confident tree?

What to watch

  • Drag an individual tree’s vote and watch the mean resist it as the forest grows.
  • Resampling changes which homes repeat or disappear from each bootstrap sample.
  • Low feature diversity makes tree opinions more correlated and less useful to average.
  • More trees reduce variance, but do not automatically remove shared bias.

Why the trees disagree

Each tree gets a remixed training set and a limited menu of questions.

Bootstrap sampling means drawing training homes at random with replacement. A home can appear more than once in one tree’s sample, while another home may be left out. Then, at each split, the tree considers only a random subset of features. These two sources of randomness keep the trees from becoming identical copies.

Diversity is the point: averaging helps most when the trees make different mistakes. If every tree learned the same bias, twenty-five matching opinions would still be one opinion repeated.

How it is built

Remix the data. Grow the trees. Average the answers.

A random forest does not blend tree structures together. Each tree remains a complete model; the ensemble combines only their final numeric predictions.

01 · Bootstrap a training set
Dt ∼ sample(D, n, replace)

Dt (“D sub t”) is the training set for tree t. It draws n examples from the original dataset D, allowing repeats.

02 · Let one tree predict
ŷt(x) = ht(x)

ŷt (“y-hat sub t”) is tree t’s energy prediction for home conditions x. ht is that tree’s learned set of splits.

03 · Average all T trees
ŷforest = 1T Σt=1T ŷt(x)

T is the number of trees. Σ (“capital sigma”) adds their visible predictions; dividing by T produces the forest mean shown in the lab.

Key terms

The vocabulary of collective judgment.

The forest becomes more stable by creating variation among its members, then averaging away part of that variation.

Ensemble
A model made by combining the outputs of multiple component models.
Random forest
An ensemble of decision trees trained with random examples and random feature choices.
Bootstrap sample
A same-sized random sample drawn with replacement, so examples may repeat or be omitted.
With replacement
After an example is drawn, it remains eligible to be drawn again.
Feature subsampling
Offering each split only a random subset of the available input features.
Variance
How much a model’s prediction changes when its training data changes slightly. Averaging varied trees usually reduces it.
Bias
A consistent error caused by assumptions shared across the model or forest.
Forest prediction
For regression, the arithmetic mean of the individual tree predictions.
