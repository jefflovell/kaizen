Kaizen is a journey of improvement one step at a time.

Kaizen (改善) is Japanese for achieving a state of continuous improvement. Popularized as the central business philosophy of the Toyota Way, it is the practice of small, deliberate, and measurable changes compounding into durable improvements.

This site is part of my practice of that philosophy: a constant, work-in-progress collection of field notes and hard-won lessons, professional philosophy and personal musings, experiments and explainers attempting to demystify complex systems and build intuitive mental models.

My goal is simple but challenging: to provide the resource I wish I had for making complicated topics feel approachable while leaving enough breadcrumbs for deeper exploration. In the process, I clarify my own mental models, enrich my own understanding, and improve my ability to communicate complex concepts to any audience.

My perspective is forged from years of work at move-fast-and-break-things tech startups and lumbering Fortune 50 enterprise giants. I have helped bring hundreds of products to market across music, film, television, entertainment, B2B, and B2C in roles spanning technical product management, software engineering, UI and UX design, technical program management, machine learning, business operations, and organizational design.

I hope you find value in the effort. Feel free to drop feedback and requests on GitHub, and cheers to achieving kaizen on whatever journey of improvement you are traveling.

Field note 01

Machine learning, without all the heavy machinery.

Epic

As an ML novice, I want an approachable way to learn ML that allows me to explore it from various perspectives without hiding the technicals so that I can gain a practical foundation of the subject.

Story Points
21
Status
IN PROGRESS
Assignee
Jeff Lovell

The goal is not to make machine learning sound smaller than it is. The goal is to make the machinery visible without making the reader feel like they have wandered into a math textbook with locked doors.

Each note tries to start from the learner's side of the glass: what you may already know, what the model actually computes, where the shorthand comes from, and what to trust only after testing it.
